Existential time

Today marks a year since I started this full time (and 8 months since I pivoted). I gave myself until the end of July 2025 to find traction, I did this because:

  1. that felt like a long enough time to figure out if this was a business

  2. It is a long time to work without a salary, and I knew it would take time afterwards to find a job


I knew it would be tempting to keep working on something I cared about and I didn’t want this to turn into an expensive hobby and downward economic spiral.


So far, I have not found the traction I hoped for. I wanted 2 paying customers and right now I have one free customer. I know that there are at least 2 companies out there that offer a similar service, so it seems to me that the failure to find customers is not about the product. It’s about me and my efforts/abilities/connections in marketing.


Because I focused on the pilot, I have not personally done anything to gain new customers over the last month. So, I am giving myself another month to focus on that. With a planned family vacation, this extends the deadline until mid-September. Not the sunk cost fallacy, but now that this service is so functional, the extra month feels like a reasonable investment. Keep me honest, though—no more extensions without traction.

What is SageVoice now?

It’s been awhile, so I’m going to lay out what I think SageVoice is.

Our core value proposition,

We solve interviewing subjects at scale, for clinical researchers, by offering structured interviews using AI-enabled telephone calls.


Within that, it’s clear that study recruitment is a clear need within the clinical research community. So is effective data collection. I’m working on both, because they have different success timelines.


Our Pilot

Our primary pilot, in close partnership with Dr. Sachin Agarwal and his team at the University of Columbia Medical Center, involves a cognitive assessment conducted over the phone. Today, administration of the test requires significant training and then assessment of the trainers to ensure that the scoring is done correctly. The test itself is just a measurement tool, so nobody wants to spend this much time on it.


We’ll be looking at what it’s like to administer the test using SageVoice and how SageVoice scores the test compared to humans. The bot will call 15 patients, walk them through the test, and then score it. It will also score tests conducted by humans. The hope is that SageVoice will save time and score consistently enough to avoid a mountain of frustrating effort by the research coordinators.


Did we overspecialize?

The test is a 40 minute series of 9 activities. In retrospect, I know that this is not the optimal use of an AI bot, because administration of the test has to be very precise and consistent.  AI is great at free-flowing conversation, but this test requires very specific sequences and sometimes just one-word answers. The bot can diagnose depression based on a conversation much more effectively than it can consistently deliver a complex set of word prompts. Similarly, most phone calls are less than 15 minutes–a 40 minute phone call is much harder to do.  


In building out this complicated series of timed and sequenced activities, I think we have built a system with a rich set of capabilities. On the one hand, I doubt there is another diagnostic bot that can do what ours does and this could be a niche offering.


On the other hand, the clearer market play is patient recruitment and, in pursuing this, we have built far more than we need in one direction and neglected another one that has more short-term potential.
