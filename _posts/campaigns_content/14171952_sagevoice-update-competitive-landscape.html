Competitive landscape

Starting with this week’s email, I’m going to think out loud about different aspects of this business, starting with the competitive landscape. Other topics that I’m excited to tackle soon are ethics, market size, and differentiation.



SageVoice Value Proposition (reminder)

We solve interviewing subjects at scale, for clinical researchers, by offering structured interviews using AI-enabled telephone calls.


Are we still looking at other use cases? Yes. I’d say about 10% of my time is still exploring healthcare or aging in place…it’s partly leftovers, and partly continued resonance


Personal update

I originally built this as an interview to entertain my Dad, who has dementia. He finally started picking up the phone and talking to the bot for as long as an hour at a time (his phone blocks non-contacts).  It asks him about his childhood and he repeats himself for an hour. With us, he never talks that long.


He knows it’s not a human, but he told me he thinks Sage is great. His approval shouldn’t mean anything at this point, but it does.

Again, I 99% abandoned this use case because I couldn’t get anyone I didn’t know to try it, but glad it made it to him.


What’s the competition like?

This is a great question and I’ve recently had folks share examples of voice bots or research platforms.


I think if you have an immediate advantage, such as industry experience, strong technical edge or solid entrepreneurial track record, you can raise a lot of money and tackle a huge market, like “voice agents for healthcare”.  Hippocraticai.com is a good example of that.  They take on a huge industry, across a huge number of use cases, and are throwing themselves into a competition with many dozens of companies.


I’m coming at it from a different angle.  Because software can be a winner-take-most game, at this stage, I’d rather be a big fish in a small pond than a small fish in a big pond. Bootstrapped and without insider knowledge, I’m starting with a niche that is big enough for a successful company, but not so big or easy that it’s an obvious place for the bigger fish (great article on this). That’s why “voice companions” was not appealing, but “voice companions for people with dementia” was.


The same is true here. In the case of the tech-enabled research market, you have two core dimensions and a few verticals that are adjacent.


Here is my rough assessment of the relative saturation of these markets (these sizes are rough swags for illustrative purposes only).  


According to my rough research, medical/clinical/academic research is smaller, but there are fewer big players here.


In fact, if you get into the medical research vertical, there might be sub-verticals that I want to specialize in, whether it’s by subject area (oncology v. psychiatry) or by need (subject screening, data collection or protocol adherence).  The idea is that once you do one thing better than anyone, then you can expand your use case and your market.


What do you think? Did I get something wrong here?
